CitiBike

CitiBike Data Analysis: Identifying new business opportunities.

I conducted an in-depth analysis of the CitiBike dataset to uncover opportunities for strengthening their business model and enhancing the customer experience. The findings highlighted key patterns in user demographics and behaviors, providing clear direction on where to allocate resources for maximum return.

Tools: Google Sheets, My SQL, Flourish
Deliverables: Data Story
Project Type: Data analysis project
Team: Data Analyst (me)

Business Goal

The goal was to analyze and compare the behaviors of one-time riders and long-term subscribers of NYC CitiBike. Understanding their trip frequency, duration, locations, and peak usage times will reveal bike demand hotspots.

These insights will help optimize bike distribution, refine station placement, and guide targeted marketing—such as commuter plans for weekday riders or discounts for tourists—supporting data-driven decisions that improve efficiency, satisfaction, and growth.

User Benefit

Based on our analysis, the CitiBike customer base is mostly long-term subscribers aged between 35-44, who are most active during the week. This tells us that they are probably people who live in New York (especially in Grove St Path, Sip Ave, Newport Path, Newark Ave, and Van Vorst Park) and use NY CitiBikes to commute.

Marketing and advertising campaigns should therefore target this particular demographic and more bikes should be installed at those locations.

Process

The goal of this personal project was to analyze CitiBike usage data to uncover meaningful insights about rider behavior and bike demand across New York City. I followed a structured, end-to-end data analysis workflow to simulate how a professional analyst might approach a real-world problem.

01. Define Question

At the start of this project, I had to define a few key questions:

  1. What are the most popular pick-up locations across the city for NY CitiBike rental?
  2. How does the average trip duration vary across different age groups?
  3. Which age group rents the most bikes?
  4. How does bike rental vary across the two user groups (one-time users vs long-term subscribers) on different days of the week?
  5. Does user age impact the average bike trip duration?

02. Collect Data

Although this was a personal project, I aimed to challenge myself by working with a large, authentic dataset from a real-world company rather than a simplified sample. During my search for a suitable dataset, I came across a comprehensive tutorial by CareerFoundry that featured the official CitiBike dataset. This resource not only provided the raw data I needed but also offered valuable context and guidance on how the data is typically analyzed in the industry. Using this dataset allowed me to simulate a more professional, real-world analysis, enhancing both the scope and credibility of my project.

03. Clean Data

Cleaning your data ensures accuracy and reliability before analysis. Key steps include:

  1. Delete unnecessary columns: Remove irrelevant fields (e.g., student ID or date of birth).
  2. Remove duplicates: Eliminate repeated entries from data collection errors.
  3. Handle missing data: Delete rows, impute values, or flag entries as “missing.”
  4. Address outliers: Identify unusual values and decide if they should be kept or removed.
  5. Fix inconsistencies: Standardize typos, formats, or naming conventions using tools like “Find and replace.”

04. Analyze Data

Before diving into deeper analysis, I began by getting familiar with the CitiBike dataset—taking time to understand its structure and overall layout. I approached this stage as exploratory data analysis (EDA), treating it as my first investigation of the data’s main characteristics.

Through EDA, I summarized key features, looked for emerging patterns and trends, and flagged any anomalies that might distort the results. I visualized preliminary findings with basic charts to get an intuitive sense of usage patterns and potential hotspots.

This foundation guided my next steps, ensuring that subsequent analysis was both targeted and meaningful.

05. Create Visualizations

For this project, I used data visualization to turn raw CitiBike data into clear, actionable insights. By creating graphs, charts, and maps, I highlighted key patterns, trends, and outliers that weren’t obvious from the raw numbers. Visualization wasn’t just about making the data look appealing—it made the insights understandable and meaningful for a broader audience. This approach allowed me to communicate my findings effectively so stakeholders could act on them without needing deep data expertise.

06. Share Findings

For this personal project, I envisioned CitiBike stakeholders—marketing, product, operations, and leadership—as my audience. Insights on rider behavior could inform targeted marketing, station placement, and resource allocation. Though not part of the final presentation, keeping this audience in mind helped focus my analysis and make the findings more relevant.
