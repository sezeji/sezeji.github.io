Guidehouse

Providing real-time, trusted COVID-19 data and local resources for communities.

In order to help communities stay informed and supported during a global health crisis, The National Institutes of Health needed a centralized, reliable way to deliver localized COVID-19 information and resources. To address misinformation, fragmented services, and accessibility gaps, we designed an intuitive web app that consolidates guidance, testing and vaccination locators, and support resources into one clear, user-friendly platform. The result empowered individuals to make informed decisions, reduce anxiety, and quickly access critical services during rapidly changing conditions.

Tools: Figma, Figjam
Deliverables: High fidelity mockup
Project Type: UI/UX Design
Team: UX Designer (me), Product Owner, Dev Team

Business Goal

NIH needed a reliable platform to cut through information overload and misinformation, making up-to-date, localized COVID-19 guidance and resources instantly accessible. Consolidating fragmented services into a single, trustworthy hub would minimize public confusion, support community health, and improve response efficiency.

User Benefit

For users, the goal was to provide instant confidence and clarity—allowing them to quickly check current guidelines, find nearby testing or vaccination options, and access support services without wading through multiple confusing sites. The experience reduced anxiety, saved time, and empowered better decisions during an uncertain time.

Process

I focused on creating a clear, trustworthy experience that made accessing real-time COVID-19 data and resources effortless for communities—helping users quickly find local guidance, locate testing or vaccination sites, and confidently make informed decisions during rapidly changing conditions.

01. Product Definition

We were tasked with creating a web app that delivers localized COVID-19 information, testing and vaccination locators, and support resources in a single, easy-to-navigate hub—helping users stay informed, access care, and reduce stress during a fast-changing crisis.

02. Research

We conducted online surveys, interviews with healthcare workers, parents, and seniors, and competitor analysis of health websites. Users emphasized the need for trustworthy, localized data, simple language, and appointment booking. They were frustrated by navigating multiple sites and uncertain about which information to trust.

03. Analysis

Findings revealed critical gaps: fragmented resources, confusing medical jargon, and lack of transparency increased anxiety and delayed action. We distilled these insights into key requirements: a centralized dashboard, location-aware updates, clear calls to action, and visible last-updated timestamps to build trust.

04. Design

We created wireframes and prototypes focusing on clarity and accessibility. A clean, calming visual design used cool blues and greens, legible typography, and universal icons. Features included a personalized dashboard, map-based testing locator, resource hub, and opt-in notifications. All designs met WCAG 2.1 AA standards and were responsive across devices.

05. Testing

Two rounds of usability testing refined navigation and language. Users praised the map-based locator and requested more prominent placement for guidelines. Simplified terms and clear timestamps increased trust and satisfaction. Iterations ensured tasks like “Find a nearby PCR test” or “Check current travel rules” could be completed quickly and confidently.

06. Implementation

We collaborated with NIH stakeholders and engineers to launch the web app, incorporating user feedback into the final build. Post-launch monitoring confirmed improved access to local resources, reduced confusion, and higher engagement, demonstrating the critical role of user-centered design in public health communication.
